# “Like”: Dopamine and Gambling
Addiction comes in many forms. Gambling addiction in particular has exploded over the past few decades. But I’m not talking about in-person casinos-- the attendance has actually been going down and is increasingly unpopular among younger generations <source>. Right now, I’m not talking about legal online gambling, which has expanded, normalized, and exploded beyond imagination through apps like DraftKings (likely to be dated). Right now, I’m not talking about gambling that has pervaded the video game industry, specifically designed to target and exploit children. What I want to talk about is social media and how it creates, maintains, and exploits gambling addiction techniques among its user base, which is everyone in the world with access to the internet.

First, we must go over the history of traditional gambling gaming. 

<pinball>
Pinball used to be a wooden game with pins and holes. The ball would be shot up with with a spring and bounce off of pins to either get it in a hole worth points or to miss everything and land on the bottom, kind of like an inverted ski-ball. Paddles weren’t added until way later, which changed the game but not the pins, ball, and holes.

Pachinko took that to the next level with sending up many smaller balls at once with more intricate pin and hole patterns. It was fun to design and fun to play with more action happening in shorter intervals with a more interesting visual design and a hypnotizing flow. It was still balls and pins and holes with a relatively muted sound. But years later, a bell was added every time you got a ball in a hole. When I first saw this in action, I shuddered. In context, this is the most evil thing I’ve ever seen. It lights up a part of my brain that makes me uncomfortable.  The bell feels good. *Really* good. 

When flicking out dozens of balls on the board, the bell rings with intermittent consistency even though you’re losing money. It feels really, *really* good.
